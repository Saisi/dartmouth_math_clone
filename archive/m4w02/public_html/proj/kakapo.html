Introduction

 

The kakapo, or Strigops habroptilus, is a unique bird in a variety of ways.  Only found in New Zealand, this nocturnal creature has a life span of sixty years.  It is also the world's largest, heaviest, and only flightless parrot.  Therefore, it spends most of its time on the ground or climbing trees.  Males range from 15-30 hectares while females range from 35-50 hectares.  With a diet that consists of various plant matter (roots, seeds, fruits, bulbs, leaves, etc.), it is one of only two true herbivorous birds in New Zealand.  As a result, breeding (which normally occurs every three years) can only take place when there is ample seed production in order to have enough protein to raise their chicks. 

            The kakapo has suffered a tragic history.  The species once thrived in New Zealand with hundreds of thousands of members.  Except for a giant eagle that became extinct long ago, the kakapo had no natural predators.  Approximately a thousand years ago, with the arrival of the Polynesians, this all began to change.  The Polynesians hunted the kakapo for a number of reasons.  Their meat was one of the Polynesians principle foods and their plumage was highly valued.  Their feathers were often used to make coats or to stuff pillows and mattresses.  The Polynesians also ravaged their food source and habitat by chopping and burning down the forests where the birds lived.  In addition, the new intruders brought the Polynesian rat, 'kiore', which fed off of the kakapo's eggs and young.  The kakapo was completely unprepared for these attacks.  Living without predators for so long they had developed no defense mechanisms.  Being flightless, colorful and distinctive smelling, they were easy prey for both humans and animals.

            The situation for the kakapo only worsened with the arrival of the Europeans in 1850.  The birds were slaughtered even more as a result of hunting (the Europeans enjoyed their meat and skins as well) and clearing the forests in order to build large farms.  The animals that the Europeans introduced further depleted the numbers of the kakapo.  Predators, such as rats and cats, and food competitors, such as possums and deer nearly decimated the population of kakapo.  The low point struck in 1994 when only 47 kakapo were still in existence.

            Fortunately, help soon arrived for the kakapo.  A conservation group concerned by the dire situation of the kakapo relocated the remaining few birds to a few small predator-free islands (ranging from 320ha to 1400ha).  It is currently providing them with a supplemental diet of walnuts and almonds, looking after their breeding habits, and caring for their eggs and young chicks.  Although each island can hold up to approximately one hundred kakapo, only sixty-two birds currently remain, twenty-six of which are female.

Question 2: Modeling Small Populations

 

As there are currently very few kakapo existing, a number of factors make it difficult to accurately model the population, regardless of the amount of funding or data made available.  On the most basic level, Biologists Sort the Lessons of Fisheries Collapse offers two general reasons why modeling populations can be a largely inaccurate process.  First, one must deal with the mobile and wandering nature of the kakapo. In the case where a species, “is highly mobile and patchily distributed, [biologists] admit they’re often lucky if their population estimates for any given year are within 30% of reality” (Reading 3.1, 53).  The second problem with creating the population models is that when multiple variables change over a short period of time it is almost an arbitrary process of reassigning values to different factors in the modeling equation.  For example, the kakapo stopped being hunted, were relocated to a different island, and began receiving outside supplements of food all within a short period of time.  This makes it difficult to determine which changes in particular are responsible for any changes in population, and what the magnitude of these changes truly are.

If we explore beyond these “superficial” traits, we find that, modeling small populations, in particular, is an inherently difficult process.  Reading 3.2 states that, in theory, if population numbers are low standard modeling of the population p(t) at time t predicts that there will be an exponential equation, p’ = ap, where a is a constant that is roughly the difference between the birth and death rates.  This given equation is said to be valid whenever population numbers are low and the environment is largely unchanging from year to year. However, the article is largely concerned with depensation, which is to say there exists a certain point where the above equation no longer holds true, and the population size continues to dwindle toward extinction.  In attempting to model the kakapo population, it is essential to determine whether population dipped to depensation-affected levels.  If so, then it is possible that our equation for population growth would no longer apply to the kakapo, as they would simply whither towards extinction in their given environment.  Thus, modeling the population becomes an extremely difficult proposition.  

            Also, due to certain factors, it is possible for per-capita reproductive success to decline at low population levels.  Because of this, they predict that the populations can have multiple equilibrium points and suddenly shift from one equilibrium to another.  According to Population Dynamics of Exploited Fish stocks at Low population Levels, the main characteristic that can bring about this type of shifting, unpredictable equilibrium is if reproductive frequencies (birth rate) varies when population is small.  According to the article, such a dynamic will result if reproductive success is reduced at low population densities because to the difficulty in finding viable mates.  This phenomena, termed the Allee effect, definitely applies to the Kakapo.  Of the remaining 62 kakapo in modern times, only 26 were female, and of those 26, it was estimated that 18 were no longer young enough to be of mating age.  This, combined with the fact that Kakapo are highly territorial creatures who try encroaching upon each other’s territory, means that the few eligible females would have much trouble consistently finding males with which to mate.  The unpredictability this engenders would cause the downward “shifting” in potential equilibriums, thus making the Kakapo extremely difficult to model at low populations.

            However as the article points out, overall, “the great majority of . . . populations show evidence of increased survival at lower population levels” (Reading 3.3, 61).  This is particularly true when it becomes possible to directly stock the animal population with food and resources.  The appearing implication seems to be that the resulting reductions in mortality rates, “should enable currently remaining stocks to rebuild, unless environmental or ecosystem-level changes occur that alter the underlying dynamics of the stock”  (Reading 3.3, 61). 

Thus, modeling small populations is an inherently inexact science in which equilibriums may be constantly changing, and certain “trigger points” may render the entire equation false.  Kakapo in particular, by virtue of their low-female ratio and highly dispersed concentrations are especially difficult to model for the future with any certainty.  Thus, even for those who have plentiful funding and data, predicting the future of the kakapo still remains an inexact science.

 

Conclusion

 

According to our projections, the current effort to save the kakapo will prove to be effective in raising the population if such effort is maintained.  It will be so effective, in fact, that the population will eventually reach a steady state of 250 birds.  Because this exceeds the islands' maximum capacity of 200, the birds will have to be transferred to a new location.  Currently no such predator-free area exists we would recommend starting to search for a suitably large island and work to remove all introduced predators.  Eventually, the kakapo may even be able to be reintroduced back to the mainland.  This is the ultimate goal of the conservation project, to return the kakapo to their original habitat and for them to be self-sustaining so that human aid is not longer necessary.
