Explanation of Constants

            r, μ, ν, α are values that were taken from different sources listed in the references. C and Cm were calculated from K, μ and ν. We assumed that the capacity was the current total population currently living in sub-Sahara Africa. ( The total population living in Africa is probably much greater than the “capacity”). γ, d, and ρ were values that we found most suited the description of the malaria situation in sub-Sahara Africa.  Tm, x and y were are the birth rates of our imaginary vectors, the mosquitoes. Cm were calculated from our Tm, x and y. At the beginning of the epidemic (time= 0), we assume that E, and R are zero. S is equal  to 300 million and M is equal to 1 billion in our area of sub-Sahara Africa. I and IM are small numbers, 1 million and 1 million respectively, and they are the onset of epidemic. P is 1/8 of S at t=0. The population of P at the beginning consists only those who are have a partial immunity provided by genetics.

β is the hardest value to estimate. We have found literature and statistics that say β is anything between 1/50 to 1/10. However, we find that we can not use those values of β in our models given our limited “technology”. Instead we used a value for β that is derived from our other values in our basic equation set.

 

Explanation of Equations

            Equation 1a models the susceptible population. This group of individuals do not have the disease and are not immune so they can contract the disease. We assume that all members (S,E,In,R,P) of the population give birth to susceptible offspring at a rate of ν and that eventually those who have partial immunity by having had the disease before will eventually become susceptible again. Members leave  the population either through dying naturally at a rate of v or by becoming infected at a β. Susceptible give birth to partially immune offspring at a rate of ν*S*1/8. In Africa, approximately half the population carries at least one allele for sickle cell anemia.  That means that the chance of having a child with sickle cell anemia is about  1/8 by virtue of the Punnet square. These children are placed in the partial immune category since sickle cell anemia offers partial protection against malaria.

            Equation 1b models the exposed population. After coming in contact with an infected mosquito, susceptible people have a chance of becoming exposed at a rate of β and partially immune people have a chance of becoming exposed at a rate of β/10. After exposure there is a delay before the individual shows the first signs of malaria. During this period, the parasites are multiplying in their liver and blood but are not populous enough to 1. cause harm to the host , 2. pass on the parasite to any mosquito that happens to bite the exposed individual.  For plasmodium falciparum, it takes 8-16 days before the person is infectious and begins to show symptoms.

            Equation 1c models the infectious population. These individuals have the symptoms of malaria and can pass on the infection. They leave at a rate of μ+α+ρ. μ is the natural death rate, α is the death rate caused by malaria, and ρ is the rate at which the individuals recover.

            Equation 1d models the recovered population. After recovery, the individual has a grace period in which they are completely immune to malaria. Their bodies innate immune system recognizes malaria and will automatically kill any reintroduction of the parasite during this time period. Unfortunately, the innate immune system can not “remember” what malaria looks like so eventually over time the individual loses their immunity as the white blood cells that combated malaria gradually die and are replaced. In our model, the recovered population becomes a member of the partially immune population at a rate of γ.

            Equation 1e models the partially immune population. For simplicity, we included in this category those who are born partially immune (i.e. sickle cell anemics) and those who have had malaria, recovered and are gradually losing their immunity. This population is partially protected against malaria but still has a chance of contracting the disease, albeit at a lower rate. The subpopulation who are not genetically partially immune eventually become susceptible again at a rate of d. It is inaccurate but there is very little information and statistics on force of transmission of malaria on those who have sickle cell and those who are partially immune so we are forced to make a few assumptions.

            Equations 1f and 1g model the vectors of transmission. In the case of malaria, the vectors of transmission are mosquitoes. 1f models the mosquitoes that do not have malaria and the rate at which they can become vectors if they bite an infected individual. 1g models the infected mosquito population. Since malaria develops  in the gut of the mosquito, we know that it does not affect the reproductive rate of mosquitoes and does not transfer parasite to the offspring.

Reducing Malaria’s Infectiveness

            There are several ways to reduce malaria’s infectiveness. The susceptible hosts can be protected both by prophylaxis and by post-infection treatments. The mosquito vectors can be eliminated by  destroying their breeding places, insecticides or insect repellants. We will explore several of these methods as a way to control malaria.

 

Prophylactic treatment

            There are several drugs out there that provide protection against malaria. One of the most common medications used today for travelers going to high transmission areas is malarone. Malarone has an efficiency of 95-100% against malaria both as prophylaxis and a treatment. There are other drugs that are as effective except they have severe side effects. The drawback to malarone is its cost so its not used widely in sub-Sahara Africa where most of the countries are third world nations.

Post Infection Treatment of Malaria

            Even with a 100% effective medication, the spread of malaria continues. The reason is probably because of the vectors of transmission, the mosquitoes, are still present. They continue to expose susceptible individuals to malaria which by itself continues the epidemic.

Controlling the Spread of Malaria through Insecticides

In this model we assume that the all the mosquitoes comes in contact with the insecticide. The easiest method to insure this is to spray very small droplets of the concentrated insecticide over the any area that is known to have mosquitoes and to spray especially in mosquito breeding areas. If this is assumed then even if the insecticide is only 40% effective, it is enough to completely anihilate the mosquito population. Since the mosquitoes are the only vector for malaria, malaria stops spreading  and the epidemic ends. However, insuring that all the entire population of mosquitoes comes in contact with the insecticide is very hard task. In addition, completely annihilating the mosquito population could have undesirable consequences upon the ecosystem. Therefore we do not want to completely wipe out the mosquitoes. Though the model states that this is an effective way to stop malaria, practical considerations states otherwise. However, using a combination of insecticide  with another method like prophylaxis maybe effective in controlling and eventually stop malaria completely.

 


Fig 6: Combination Treatment of Malaria Combination Treatment of Malaria a) 20% effective insecticide, 70% effective prophylaxis treatment

 

We saw from the previous figures that both a 70 % effective prophylactic drug  and a 20% effective insecticide are not enough to control the spread of malaria by themselves. However, when used in combination, the pair is remarkably effective at treating the spread of malaria. This is just two of the methods that we just happen to model in this report. There are many different other methods that we can use to stop the spread. Assuming that this set of equations is accurate, we can use them to test the effectiveness of certain combinations of methods to find one that is the most efficient for the cost.  There are many simple cheap methods to reduce the chance of catching malaria. Insect repellant to reduce exposure to infected mosquitoes and using bed netting at night are a few of the methods. Using these equations might help find the right combination of cheaper and costlier methods that can drastically reduce the spread of malaria without bankrupting a small country.
