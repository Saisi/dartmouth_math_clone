1. What is STATISTICS?

I doubt that Joseph Stalin gave the following gloomy definition, but it reflects the essence of statistics:

Death of one man is a tragedy, deaths of millions is statistics.

Statistics deals with data, sometimes with tons of poor organized numbers (messy data). The goal of statistics is to draw inferences by analyzing the data. Statistics is a unique science because it can solve almost any real life problem where the data are available or can be collected.

Statistical approach involves four steps:

  1. Data collection (sometimes data already exist). Statistician can play a significant role on this step to design the experiment, how to sample, when to sample, etc.  In particular, he/she could help to answer the question: how much data to collect to make our future statistical inferences valid (significant).
  2. Exploratory analysis involves some basic (preliminary) statistical analysis: first graphs, basic statistical characteristics such as means, standard deviations (descriptive statistics). This is a necessary step to understand what the data are and what the data structure is.
  3. Assumption (model) formulation. One cannot make a good statistical analysis without understanding the underlying assumptions of statistical methods applied (otherwise he/she cannot justify why that specific method was chosen to analyze the data).
  4. Inferences. At this step statistical analysis is finalized and conclusions are made possibly avoiding statistical language, so that the results of statistical analysis can be understood not only by statisticians.

The role of computer is crucial in modern statistics.

 Examples:

The effect of a new treatment is under investigation. There are two groups of patients: 'control' and 'treatment'. The first group did receive the conventional treatment and the second group did receive a new treatment. The number of successes in both groups are known (data). Is the new treatment more successful than the conventional one? This is a statistical problem (please name four steps mentioned above).
You are in charge of making sales projection for the next year. The (non-statistical) question is: "should the company, where you work, built a new facility due to the increase in sales?" You collected data regarding sales and other relevant information, like general economic performance in the country, for several years prior to the prediction. What to do with this data to come up with projection? This is a statistical question (please name four steps, mentioned above).

2. Probability , Statistics, and Data Analysis

There is a close relationship between these three approaches.

Probability. Formally speaking any probability problem starts with defining a probability space, i.e. what kind of events are under consideration and what is the probability measure. The goal of the Theory of Probability is to find the probabilities of more complex events built up on the basis of elementary events. For instance, if you toss a fair dice elementary events are 1,2,3,4,5,6 with equal probability 1/6. A probabilistic question would be "what is the probability to get more than 10 in two tosses?".

Statistics is the inverse probability. Probabilities are not defined in statistical problem -- on contrary, they must be estimated from the data (that is why it is called inverse). For example, a dice was tossed one thousand times and the results to get 1,2,3,4,5,6 respectively were {143,172,156,180,203,146}. A statistical question: "Is the dice fair?" Here we do not assume the outcomes to have equal probabilities, rather we estimate these probabilities and verify if they are equal. A characteristic feature of statistics is the availability of the underlying model, i.e. before starting statistical analysis we should make assumptions on the mechanism how data have been produced. Model is expressed in the language of the theory of probability where probability measure is unknown (some parameters are unknown).

Data Analysis is a broader interpretation of statistics. Unlike statistical approach we do not require to formulate the underlying model. We just try this and that statistical method, make plots, etc, i.e. to dig out something informative and relevant to the problem we analyze. Recently it is called 'data mining'.

4. My Personal Experience in Statistics

Besides intensive theoretical research (My Work, Resume), I was involved in many applied statistical projects with broad range of goals: from long-term forecasting of American Economy and Business Management to Optimal Plasma Coating and Cancer Radiology. I just would like to stress that STATISTICS is one of the most powerful and interesting science I have ever known. Statistical analysis can make substantial contribution to the subject once you have (or you can collect) data.

Here is the list of projects I took or taking part in (or what statistics can do):

Long-term forecasting of American Economy, funded by High Soviet Authorities. Methods used: econometric models, nonlinear growth curves advanced macroeconomics, production functions and capital accumulation theory (by Sollow).
Sensitivity analysis of American Economy to the World Oil Production (Energy crisis modeling, I bet your parents still remember "No Gas" sign in the middle of seventies).
Modeling of World Market of Gold, funded by the Ministry of Foreign Trade, USSR.
Statistical methods of time series analysis and forecasting (commercial statistical package, software).
Business Simulator, software. Educational tool to improve business performance via computer simulations.
Optimal width distribution for plasma coating.
Optimal portfolio analysis, funded by the Ministry of Foreign Trade, USSR.
Cancer radiobiology. How tumor responses on radiation: modeling foundation.
Alternative methods of breast cancer imaging.
