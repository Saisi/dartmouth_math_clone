Comment: This is a fascinating piece on how people could make decisions that deviate from the rules of probability. There are two urns and both contain either red or black balls. The proportion of both kinds of balls in urn1 is unknown, but the proportion in urn2 is 50/50. When people are told that they will receive $100 if they pick a red ball, most of them decide to pick from urn2. Notice, however, whether urn1 or urn2 has a larger proportion for red balls is unknown. To make the game more interesting, we call it a "red bet" if the bet is to pick a red ball and a "black bet" if the bet is to pick a black ball. Stats show that some people choose urn2 for both a black bet and a red bet. That is to say, these people believe that there is a higher probability to pick a red ball in urn2 than in urn1, and there is a higher probability to pick a black ball in urn2 than in urn1. This perception contradicts the rules of probability.

Comment: A big problem for research in economics, sociology and medicine is the difficulty of finding a controlled experiment to work with. For example, it would be really nice to be able to randomly select half the people in your sample and force them to do another year of schooling (treatment), and take the other half and force them not to do it. Then you can estimate the effect the additional year of schooling had on their wages. The fact that the people were randomly selected eliminates the problem of self-selection and so you can really isolate the specific effect of 1 extra year of schooling. Researchers have been trying to somehow create these "natural experiments" with randomly selected people being subjected to a "treatment" and one interesting example comes from the field of medicine: a doctor in the US wanted to see if knee-surgery really helps relieve arthritis, or if it's in fact the psychological effect of having the surgery done that is at work. So he had a group of patients, all planning to get surgery. They agreed to be a part of his experiment, so he randomly divided the group in two parts: half would not go in the operating room, and the other half would. Of those that would go in the operating room, half would get real surgery, and half would just get their knee opened up (so that they couldn't tell whether they had surgery or not). The doctor randomly selected which patient would actually get the surgery and which not. He then monitored the three groups. If knee surgery is important, we would expect the guys that didn't get cut and the guys that got cut but didn;t really have the surgery to have the same outcome, and the patients who got the real thing to have better outcome. He discovered that the guys who had been cut (regardless of whether or not they had actually had proper surgery) felt better afterwards, proving the strong psychological impact of the surgery.
There are other examples of this type of created experiment, with brain surgery, heart surgery, testing a new pill, etc... nowadays the patients agree to be part of the experiment (they just don't know if they are in the treatment group or not) but 50 years ago, a guy did this with heart surgery and never told the patients....

Comment: Art history and statistics? Few people would make a connection between these two seemingly very different domains, but University of Chicago economist David Galenson has tried to do so. In his book Painting Outside the Lines: Patterns of Creativity in Modern Art, he analyzes avant-garde art from a statistical perspective. What he finds out is that there are two distinct patterns of artistic innovation, which he calls the experimental and conceptual patterns. Experimentalists such as Cezanne tend to improve their art gradually, whereas conceptualists like Picasso make sudden great improvements. Galenson measures improvement by looking at the market prices of the paintings, assuming that artistic talent and creativity are just like ordinary goods, which get a certain price based on their quality. He concludes that experimental artists produce their best paintings towards the end of their lives, whereas conceptualists tend to produce masterpieces much earlier. His approach has been criticized by many art historians for two reasons: First, it assumes a linear relationship between the quality of a painting as determined by its critical evaluation, and its market price as determined by popular demand. In other words, critics say the most sought-after paintings of a particular artist might not be his or her best works. Second, art historians doubt Galenson's claim that he can use prices to infer details about the artist and his work - for example, to "predict what the artist said about his work and how he made preparatory drawings." Art historians tend to view each artist and each work of art as a unique phenomenon, and are very skeptical about such generalized statistical laws.

Comment: In a recent court case, the reliability of fingerprinting as a method of identifying people was questioned. It was pointed out that fingerprinting does not match one of the so-called Daubert criteria for the reliability of legal evidence. In particular, its rate of error is not known. Methods to estimate the probability of two people having the same fingerprint have been designed before. The first person to propose such a method was Sir Francis Galton. He used an enlarged photograph of a fingerprint and dropped small paper squares on it. Then he tried to infer the features hidden behind each square from the surrounding pattern, which he could see. He found out that he could correctly guess the hidden features with probability 0.5 if he used squares of size 5 fingerprint ridges per edge. Then he divided the fingerprint into 24 such squares, and assuming independence, calculated the probability of guessing the entire pattern by looking at its margins to be 0.5^24. Finally, he multiplied it by the probability to have a particular set of margins and obtained that the probability of a given fingerprint pattern is 1.45 * 10^(-11). This meant that if one chose a random fingerprint, there was a chance of 1/4 of finding another person with the same fingerprint. In my opinion, this probability might be somewhat higher, since not all patterns are equally likely to occur - some might never even occur. A method that takes this into account was proposed by Osterburg et al. They developed Galton's basic idea of subdividing the fingerprint into small squares and used the experimentally observed probabilities of finding different small features (minutiae) in a given square to calculate the total probability of a particular configuration.

Comment: Trust and Growth In a paper written 5 years ago, Paul Zak and Stephenen Knack document a relationship between trust and economic growth. They use a sample of 37 countries, for a period of 22 years from 1970-1992, for which they gather data on investment, GDP growth per capita, initial GDP and investment (1970 levels), inflation and education levels (the typical variables included in growth regressions). They find that on average, trust (as measured by the World Values Survey) is higher in more ethnically, socially and economically homogeneous societies. Higher-trust societies in turn, exhibit higher rates of investment and growth, holding other economic and geographic variables constant. To estimate the correlation between trust and economic growth they use regression analysis, in an equation of the form growth = b0 + b1(trust index) + bx, where x=other control variables. The index b1 shows that for a 1-point increase in the trust index, the country would be expected to grow by an addional 0.1%, holding other parameters constant. While such results are interesting and rather intuitive (if people in a society trust each other, they are more likely to undertake efficient, profitable economic activities), I cannot help but wonder if the results suffer from what is called an endogeneity problem, with the trust index used actually being a proxy for other variables not included in the model, such as social stability, rule of law, and effective property rights and business regulations, which would in turn not only encourage economic growth, but also determine a high level of trust among people in that society.
