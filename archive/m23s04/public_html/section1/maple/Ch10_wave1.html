In what follows I denote by "Series", Series(x,t,n), the first n terms of the infinite series that gives the solution

of the wave equation. It is a function of x=position, t=time and n=number of terms. The Fourier coefficients

were calculated in class: = = .

> Series := (x,t,n) -> sum(8/(m^2*Pi^2)*sin(m*Pi/2)*sin(m*Pi*x/10)*cos(m*Pi*t/10), m=1..n); u:=plot3d([x,t,Series(x,t,100)],x=0..10,t=0..60,style=wireframe,color=blue,numpoints=1000): display(u,axes=frame,labels=["x","t","u(x,t)"]);

> animate(Series(x,t,100),x=0..10,t=0..60,frames=200,numpoints=100);

The above works well, but it requires the computation of the coefficients in advance (and introducing the results

into the command lines). There are other ways of doing the same thing. For example one can take advantage of

the fact that Maple can perform symbolic computations, in other words can find the Fourier coefficients for us.

Here are some more examples.

> L:=1: a:=1:

We first define m-th Fourier coefficient of the odd extension of :

> k:=(m,f)->2*int(f*sin(m*Pi*x/L),x=0..L);

Next we define a partial series of the solution of the wave equation. Notice that it is a function of the initial

displacement only.

> u:=f->sum(k(m,f)*sin(m*Pi*x/L)*cos(a*m*Pi*t/L),m=1..100);

> animate(u(x*(x-L)),x=0..L,t=0..5,frames=200);

> animate(u(x*(x-L)*(x-L/2)*(x-3*L/4)),x=0..L,t=0..5,frames=200);
