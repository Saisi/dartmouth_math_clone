Statistical research interest:

Methods of estimation and diagnostics for linear and nonlinear mixed effects models

  • Description: In classical regression analysis one deals with the dependent variable which may be represented as a vector (one dimension), and there is no correlation between components of that vector. In a fairly new statistical approach, called mixed effects, the dependent variable is a matrix (two dimensions). In a mixed effects model there are repeated measurements and components of the dependent variables are correlated. In particular, mixed effects modeling approach is used to analyze longitudinal data where each individual (subject or experimental unit) is observed in time. Recently two books have been published in this field: Davidian & Giltinan. Nonlinear Models for Repeated Measurement Data and Vonesh & Chinchilli (1997). Linear and Nonlinear Models for the Analysis of Repeated Measurements. This topic remains hot in statistics. I work on mixed effects and growth curves models from 1992 and published several papers, see below.

Computational statistics, optimization algorithms, uniqueness and existence of estimates

  • Description: Computations in statistics play a central role: statistics deals with data, to make statistical inference one must analyze data through major calculations which often involve optimization algorithms and iterative solutions. Statistics today is impossible without computer and relevant software. Unlike least squares estimate more complex statistical schemes lead to a non-quadratic minimization function, e.g. nonlinear regression or maximum likelihood for variance components or mixed effects model. The fundamental questions are: Does the global minimum exist (attain) and if it does how to verify that the found local minimum is, in fact, the global one. These, easy to formulate questions, puzzle statisticians and mathematicians for many years, not much progress has been done in this field, especially for the global minimum criteria. I work on these topics from 1981 and developments are promising.

Regression diagnostics: infinitesimal influence analysis

  • Description: Regression diagnostics is a part of regression analysis, and more generally robustness of statistical model estimation and inference. A major concern of regression diagnostics and influence analyses is detecting influential observations and how they affect statistical inferences. Standard regression diagnostics include the following methodology: case deletion diagnostics, Cook’s distance, leverage and standardized residuals analysis. In 1986 Dennis Cook proposed local influence approach applied to any statistical model (Journal of the Royal Statistical Society, ser. B). He suggested to measure how likelihood displacement is affected by small perturbation in data or model. We generalize that approach by considering any characteristic or estimate. Infinitesimal influence analysis can be applied virtually to any model or statistic and does not require distribution specification. Especially helpful it is for complex statistical models such as mixed effects models with repeated measurements. The advantages of the new approach are: easy of calculations, possibility of graphical representation, separated influence analysis for dependent and independent variables.

Applied statistics. How tumor responses on radiation

  • Description: There is a well established theory of statistical growth curves, exponential, logistic, Gompertz, etc. However irradiated tumor does not follow these curves because it may decline and then re-grow. Statistical modeling of perturbed tumor in vivo is a long-standing problem. I develop a general re-growth curve theory where re-growth contains the radiation dose as a parameter. The idea is to combine the two established theories, growth curve and dose-response relationship (called survival curves in tumor and cancer literature) using cell kinetics modeling. An adequate tumor re-growth curve may provide a powerful quantitative tool to contrast different radiation schemes, optimal fractionation, probability of tumor control for different tumor models.

 

Software:

I designed and programmed several software (theoretical research comes to computation, sooner or later!):

  • GROWTH: Menu driven statistical program for estimation of linear and nonlinear mixed effects growth curves models (co-authored with T.Stukel, Dartmouth Medical School).
  • BUSINESS SIMULATOR: Tools of management by professional managers for managers (Gadzinski & Associates, demo is available). How current business decisions affect manufacture company future.
  • TREND: Commercial statistical package for time series analysis and forecasting, distributed by the company 'MATHEMATIKA' in Russia (demo is available, view some screenshots 1,2,3,4,5).
  • CURRENCY: Interactive software program for optimal portfolio analysis (funded by The Ministry of Foreign Trade, USSR). How to determine an optimal ‘currency basket’ which minimizes the risk due to possible currency fluctuations.
  • OPTCOAT: Optimization of Thermal Spray Coating Formation. What is the width distribution after several coatings and how to find an optimal number of passes.

 

Major publications:

  • Demidenko E. (1981). Linear and Nonlinear Regression (in Russian), Moscow, Finansi and Statistika.
    This book concerns with analysis and discussion of standard regression model and alternative regression schemes such as regression with stochastic independent (explanatory) variables, robust and M- regression, regression under multicollinearity and ridge regression, errors-in-variables, heteroscedastic residuals, and nonlinear regression.
    Table of contents.
  • Demidenko E. (1989). Optimization and Regression (in Russian), Moscow, Nauka.
    Many optimization problems emerge from statistics and data analysis. Optimization is an important part of statistical calculations and nonlinear regression particularly. This book considers the problem of sum of squares of residuals minimization in detail. Three major topics are discussed: Existence, Uniqueness, Calculation. In nonlinear regression, unlike linear regression, the Least Squares estimate may not exists -- we provide some criteria for LS estimate existence. Again, unlike linear regression, the Sum of Squares may have several local minima – we provide sufficient criteria to verify whether the found local minimum is the global one, i.e., corresponds to the true LS estimate. Different algorithms for the SS minimizations and other related computational issues are also considered.
    Table of contents.
