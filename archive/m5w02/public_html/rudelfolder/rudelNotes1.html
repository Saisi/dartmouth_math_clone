            In life, and hence in mathematics, we often use the notion of “equivalence classes,” which are sets of things that we are willing to say are all equivalent for whatever purposes we have in mind.  Here are some examples:

 

            A)1, 3/3, .999999…,(2-1),…   are all equivalent for most purposes, as they all represent the same number, the number we commonly describe as “1.”  Note that, while these are all equivalent if one only cares about the real number represented, they are not at all equivalent if one cares about typesetting.

 

            B) “napes,” “paens,” and “panes,” are all equivalent if one cares only about what letters are being used, but they would not be eqivalent for many other purposes.

 

C)      Any two equilateral triangles are equivalent if you only care about the ratios of the sides, but they would not be equivalent if one cared about size.

 

 

In each of the above we have an example of a set of things that, for one reason or another, we are willing to consider all “the same.”  We readily consider “3/3” and 2/2” as being indistinguishable for purposes of algebra.  When we say a statement such as “1 is the smallest positive integer,” what we mean is that “[1] is the smallest positive integer.” Where by [1] I mean anything that we consider equivalent to 1.  It is nothing special about the typesetting that makes “1” the smallest positive integer.

 

We will often find that there is a relation that defines when objects are equivalent.  For example, when considering elements modulo 10, we know that 13 and 43 are equivalent.  So we say that 13 º 43.  We then say that two things “a” and “b” are equivalent if a º b.  Sometimes we find that this relation is based on what we don’t care about.  For example, in modulo 10 we say “We are pretending that 10 is equivalent to 0, so you can feely add or subtract 10 from any element and get an element that is equivalent to it.”  In this case I could start with 13 and add 10 (the thing we say doesn’t matter) to it three times to get 43.  Similarly, if I said “rearrangement of letters doesn’t matter to me,” we get “panes” º “napes” º “paens.”  One last example of this would be saying “Uniformly enlarging or uniformly shrinking doesn’t matter,” which would say any two triangles are congruent [though in High School we called this “similar.”] if they have the same angle measurements.

 

In some ways we can speak of these as “allowed transformations.”  Actions we are allowed to perform without changing equivalence classes.  For example, when considering numbers modulo n the “allowed transformation” is “add or subtract n.”  Notice that we do not need to day “add or subtract any multiple of n,” because we are allowed to do as many transformations as we want, so we can simply keep adding n until we have added it the number of times we desire.

 

 

Another example would be the idea of shapes in the plane, and two shapes being the same if one is a magnification of the other…in this case the “allowable transformations” are stretching and shrinking…as we are allowed to stretch or shrink and stay within the same equivalence class.

 

I will use the notation [a] to represent “all the things equivalent to ‘a’.”  So, in modulo 7, [4]={4,11,18,25,…-3,-10,…}  Similarly, [11] also equals {4,11,18,25, …,-3,-10,…}.

 

Now, I would like you to get some practice with this sort of thing.  I will give an example of a large set of things.  I will also give a “allowable transformation,” and an equivalence class.  You should figure out what elements are in the equivalence class.

 

Let S be the set of rational numbers with positive numerator and denominator.  Suppose the “allowable transformation” is “you can add one to the denominator and subtract one from the numerator or vice versa.”

 

What is [4/3]?

 

Let S be the set of three digit numbers where the “allowable transformation” is “you may switch any two digits if they differ by 1.”

 

What is [346]?

What is [237]?

 

Let S be the set of 5 letter English words and the “allowable transformation” is “you may change any vowel into any other vowel.”

 

What is [pores]?

What is [moth]?
