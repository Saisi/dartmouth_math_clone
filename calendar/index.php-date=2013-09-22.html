Isometry groups of homogeneous Riemannian manifolds
Carolyn Gordon, Dartmouth

16:15 Graduate Student Seminar, Kemeny 108  
Combinatorial species and graph enumeration
Justin Troyka
One of the major projects of combinatorics is to count all the graphs with a given property. In some settings, it is important to think about these graphs with labels on them, but in other settings only the unlabeled "shapes" of the graphs are important. I will explain the basics of the theory of combinatorial species, a novel toolset which provides a rigorous foundation for dealing with this distinction. I'll then show how this theory can be used to solve open problems in graph enumeration, including counting the point-determining bipartite graphs. This is the topic that I studied for my senior comprehensive project in college.

14:30 Number Theory Seminar, 343 Kemeny  
L-functions, modular forms, and arithmetic
Jim Brown, Clemson University
Abstract: As will be illuminated in the colloquium following this seminar, there is a long history of using complex analysis to study problems that arise in arithmetic. One prominent such occurrence is the use of L-functions. (The Riemann zeta function is an L-function.) Given most any geometric object of interest in number theory, there is an associated L-function. By looking at ``special'' values of this function one often gains deep arithmetic insight into the geometric object one started with. In this talk I will briefly recall a couple of well-known particular cases of this phenomenon before moving on to recent joint work with Mahesh Agarwal on the Bloch-Kato conjecture for elliptic newforms of square-free level. We show up to some reasonable technical hypotheses one direction of the conjecture is true for a particular twist of the motive associated to the newform.
16:00 Math Colloquium, tba  
Prime factorization, complex analysis, and applications to arithmetic geometry
James Brown, Clemson University
Given a positive integer n, one knows that there is a unique prime factorization of n. However, in more general rings determining when one has unique factorization is a very difficult problem. As there are cases when one cannot factor elements uniquely into primes
(try 6 in Z[-5]), one would also like some way to measure how far away from having unique factorization a ring is. The class group of a ring is a group that measures the failure of unique factorization. We will discuss some results about the class group that relate back to Fermat's last theorem, before moving on to more delicate results on divisibility of class groups. We will use the case of class groups of number fields to motivate more general results dealing with modular forms and Galois representations.
