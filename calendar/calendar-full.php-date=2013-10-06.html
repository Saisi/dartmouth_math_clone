Tuesday, October 8
9:00 AM Functional Analysis Seminar, Kemeny 307
Danny Crytser, Dartmouth College
Stability for Ultragraph Algebras
4:00 PM Geometry and Topology Seminar, Geometry Lab (307 Kemeny Hall)
Craig J. Sutton, Dartmouth College
Can you hear the length spectrum of a symmetric spaces?
ABSTRACT: Motivated in part by considerations from quantum mechanics, it is a long-standing folk-conjecture that the spectrum of a manifold determines its length spectrum. This conjecture is known to be true for sufficiently ``bumpy'' manifolds; however, our understanding in the homogeneous setting---where closed geodesics come in large families---is rather incomplete. In this talk we will discuss our efforts to use wave trace techniques to explore the validity of this conjecture in the case of compact symmetric spaces. In particular, we will confirm the conjecture for classical Lie groups equipped with a bi-invariant metric by showing that the Poisson relation is an equality.
 
Wednesday, October 9
4:15 PM Graduate Student Seminar, Kemeny 108
Dan Cianci, Dartmouth College
On Representations of Matrix Groups
Representation Theory is a branch of mathematics in which a group is modelled as a subgroup of GL(V) for some vector space V. In this talk I will introduce some basic concepts in representation theory to study irreducible representations of the matrix groups SU(2) and SO(3). We will use the irreducible representations of SO(3) (and some high-powered representation theory) to deduce the spectrum of the Laplace operator on S^2.
 
Thursday, October 10
2:30 PM Number Theory Seminar, Kemeny 343
John Miller, Rutgers University
Upper bounds on class numbers and applications to cyclotomic fields.
Surprisingly, the class number of cyclotomic fields has only been determined for fields of rather small conductor, due to the difficulty of finding the "plus part" of the class number, i.e. the class number of the maximal real subfield. For example, prior to our recent work, the class number of the real cyclotomic fields of prime conductor had only been calculated for primes up to 67. By studying the prime ideals of the field, we establish class number upper bounds, allowing us to determine the class number for real cyclotomic fields of larger conductor than has been previously possible.
4:00 PM Math Colloquium, 008 Kemeny
Nathaniel Brown, Pennsylvania State University
The structure of simple operator algebras
The theory of operator algebras is relatively new, introduced less than 100 years ago to accommodate quantum mechanics. Since then the field has made deep connections with many areas of mathematics, including geometry, topology, probability, numerical analysis, dynamical systems and more. Just like in group theory, the simple operator algebras (those without ideals, in the ring theoretic sense) play a distinguished role and many deep parallels and analogies have recently been discovered. I will start at the beginning, with definitions and several important examples of operator algebras. Then I will explain the main discoveries of the 1970s (due to Alain Connes and others), and some of the amazing recent work (due to Wilhelm Winter and others) that points to a very bright future.

Friday, October 11
9:00 AM Functional Analysis Seminar, Kemeny 307
Danny Crytser, Dartmouth College
Stability for Ultragraph Algebras II
3:00 PM Applied and Computational Mathematics Seminar, Carpenter 013
Christoph Borgers, Tufts University
What is computational neuroscience, and is it good for anything?
I study differential equations models of nerve cells and brain networks. I don't think that anybody knows, at this point, whether that sort of work will prove to be a significant part of understanding the brain. I will give examples of issues I have thought and written about, mostly concerning gamma oscillations, i.e., approximately 40 Hz oscillations in electrical fields in the brain.
