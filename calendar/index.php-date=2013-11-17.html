16:15 Graduate Student Seminar, Kemeny 108  
The Reverse Mathematics of Sequential Finite Combinatorics
Seth Harris, Dartmouth College
Many finite combinatorial statements are of the form "for all sets X there exists a set Y such that phi(X,Y)." The sequential form of this statement is "for all sequences of sets (X_n)_n there exists a sequence of sets (Y_n)_n such that for all n phi(X_n, Y_n). After an introduction to the reverse mathematics program, we will use reverse mathematics to find the proof-theoretic strength of some of these sequential forms. This will tell us, among other things, whether a uniform proof exists of the finite statement.

16:00 Math Colloquium, 008 Kemeny  
Information diffusion in complex networks
Ernesto Estrada, University of Strathclyde, Glasgow
I will give a gentle introduction to the topic of complex networks. Starting from the Milgram experiment and the concept of 'Small-World', I will motivate the necessity for defining some metrics that characterize the ways in which 'information' is diffused in such complex networked systems. Then, I will introduce the concept of network communicability and illustrate the use of matrix functions for its quantitative characterization. I will then introduce a new metric, called the communicability distance. It characterizes the distance between two nodes in terms of the routes used by information to diffuse from one place to another in the network. Finally, I will extend these concepts to study networks evolving in time as well as networks where the information flows in parallel layers (multiplexes).
