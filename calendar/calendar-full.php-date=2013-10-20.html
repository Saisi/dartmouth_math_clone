Tuesday, October 22
9:00 AM Functional Analysis Seminar, Kemeny 307
Scott Smedinghoff, Dartmouth College
Hilbert Modules
2:00 PM Applied and Computational Mathematics Seminar, Kemeny 201
Lin Zhao, Dartmouth College
Computing Laplacian Eigenvalues using the Fredholm Determinant of a Boundary Integral Operator
Numerical computation of the eigenvalues of the Laplacian to high accuracy is known to be difficult. The boundary integral method has been used to tackle this problem, turning it into a nonlinear eigenvalue problem. We present a method of computing eigenvalues of the planar Dirichlet Laplacian by first constructing the Fredholm determinant of a boundary integral operator using the Nystrom method with product quadrature to handle weakly singular kernels, then solving for zeros through Chebyshev expansion and polynomial root finding. Singular values are also used to obtain high accuracy at close eigenfrequencies. An exterior resonance can occur for concave domains which results in spurious eigenvalues. We overcome this by using a combined layer potential. Our analysis gives exponential convergence rate for analytic domains.
4:00 PM Geometry and Topology Seminar, Geometry Lab (307 Kemeny)
Ori Parzanchevsky, Institute for Advanced Study
High dimensional spectrum, homology and random walks
Abstract: I will describe two connections between the spectrum of a simplicial complex and its geometry. The first is a generalization of Cheeger's inequality, and the second is a stochastic process which reflects the existence of high-dimensional homology. Both generalize well known phenomena from graph theory, and present the challenge of finding parallel behaviors in the Riemannian settings. Based on joint work with Ron Rosenthal.
 
Wednesday, October 23
4:15 PM Graduate Student Seminar, Kemeny 108
Danny Crytser, Dartmouth College
Adjoint functors arise everywhere
Category theory was originally invented to give a suitable framework for discussing natural equivalences and natural transformations between functors. As people studied it throughout the 20th century, adjoint functors and universal mapping properties became more prominent. Adjoint functors are interesting for at least two reasons. 1) If you know that a functor is a left or right adjoint, then you automatically know that it preserves limits or colimits, respectively (think of these like products or coproducts, but more general). 2) Adjoint functors allow you to describe potentially complicated Hom-sets in one category by simpler Hom-sets in another category (this is most recognizable in free-forgetful adjunction and Hom-tensor adjunction). I'll discuss the different ways you can define adjoints, common examples, and perhaps look at the concept of a Kan extension, of which Saunders Maclane modestly said "The notion of a Kan extension subsumes all the other fundamental concepts of category theory."

Ori Parzanchevski, Institute for Advanced Study
The spectral theory of complexes
While the Laplacian of a discrete graph is well known and studied, too little attention has been given so far to its high dimensional sibling â€” the Laplacian of a simplicial complex. This operator can be thought of as a discrete analogue of the Hodge Laplacian in Riemannian geometry, but at the same time it is very easy to define and compute, being a finite dimensional linear transformation. I plan to give a survey of some of the topological, geometric, and combinatorial information the simplicial Laplacian carries on a complex. No prior knowledge is assumed.
