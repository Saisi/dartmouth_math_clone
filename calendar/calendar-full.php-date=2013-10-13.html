Monday, October 14
6:00 PM SIAM Chapter Event, Kemeny 004
John Cain, Harvard University
Chaos Control and Cardiac Arrhythmia
Abstract: When a truck load of chickens collides with a truck load of molasses on a busy interstate, the result is chaos. But how would applied mathematicians define "chaos" in a more rigorous sense? Where might chaotic systems be observed in nature? In particular, what would happen if the electrical wave patterns in the heart become "chaotic"? In this presentation, I'll partially answer these questions. I'll give an example of an abnormal cardiac rhythm that can degrade into a "chaotic" rhythm that can be fatal within minutes. Then, I'll discuss some of the basic mathematics behind how that rhythm could be controlled using a medical device. I'll keep the mathematical content of this talk at a level accessible to undergraduates.
 
Tuesday, October 15
2:00 PM Applied and Computational Mathematics Seminar, Kemeny 201
Farran Briggs, Geisel School of Medicine, Dartmouth College
Attention and signal-to-noise in neural circuits
There are two questions I'm particularly interested in addressing: 1) how does the brain overcome signal redundancy resulting from anatomically divergent connections; and 2) how does attention change the activity of neurons in the brain. My results suggest that the cellular mechanisms by which attention alters neuronal activity may actually also help reduce signal redundancy in specific anatomical circuits that have high divergence (thalamocortical circuits). I will detail our experimental methods for identifying and recording from connected neurons participating in thalamocortical circuits in awake-behaving animals performing an attention task. I will then describe our results which indicate that attention increases the efficacy of neural communication and also increases signal-to-noise in these circuits. Interestingly, the manner by which attention increases signal-to-noise may also help overcome signal redundancy in highly divergent thalamocortical networks.

Wednesday, October 16
4:15 PM Graduate Student Seminar, Kemeny 108
Emma Chiappetta, Dartmouth College
Distinguished Riemannian Metrics
In this talk we will explore the question, "Does every manifold admit a 'best metric'?" We will review some basic concepts from Riemannian geometry. Then, the majority of the talk will be devoted to motivating what is meant by "best". We will argue that Einstein metrics should be the primary object of study in this field and, finally, survey some related results.
 
Thursday, October 17
4:00 PM Math Colloquium, 008 Kemeny
Jonathan S. Bloom, Dartmouth College
From pattern avoidance to rectangular Young tableaux: two new results
First, we discuss the enumerative consequences of a new bijection to the field of pattern avoidance. Although this mapping, Π, was originally defined in order to answer a question about shape-Wilf-equivalence, it has since unified (and simplified the proofs of) many existing results in the field. Most notable among these is a simplification for the enumeration of 2314-avoiding permutations. Previously, M. Boná showed via a complicated bijection that these permutations were enumerated by a certain algebraic generating function. We will demonstrate how this enumeration can easily be obtained via the mapping Π. Further, we establish new enumerative results using Π in the context of rook placements, perfect matchings, and set partitions.

On a very different note, we also discuss a recent proof confirming a conjecture of J. Propp and T. Roby regarding the new idea of homomesy. Let X be a set of combinatorial objects, G a cyclic group acting on X, and f : X → C. We say the triple ( X, G, f ) exhibits homomesy if the average value of f on any two orbits is the same. In particular, the Propp--Roby conjecture stated that rectangular Young tableaux under promotion (an operation originally defined by Shützenberger) exhibits homomesy. We will define these terms and if time permits provide two proofs of this result: the first using Fomin's growth diagrams and the second involving jeu de taquin.
