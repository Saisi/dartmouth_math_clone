Abstract: The Hasse Principle is an important concept in algebraic number theory which binds global and local theories together. In the context of solving equations, it relates solvability of an equation defined over the rationals to its solvability over the reals and over all the p-adic fields. The Hasse principle holds in many settings (e.g. quadratic forms) as witnessed by important theorems: Hasse-Minkowski, Hasse Norm, Albert-Brauer-Hasse-Noether. Well, you see where the name of the principle comes from. In a recent Monthly article, counterexamples to the Hasse principle were discussed. We describe the Hasse principle, give examples, and then explore a famous counterexample of Selmer.
