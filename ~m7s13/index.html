Rationale

What are mathematical puzzles? What purpose do they serve? How have they been passed from generation to generation, sometimes for millennia? What makes a great puzzle? The subject matter of this course is not something you will find anywhere else in the mathematics curriculum, and indeed, it is neither required for, nor requires, any other mathematics course. Nonetheless, the problem-solving and problem-posing skills you sharpen in this course will help you not only in mathematics and the sciences, but in all disciplines. In particular, the ability to state a problem clearly in writing, and to explain its solution in way that any reader can understand, will help you to be both precise and persuasive in all your communication.


Course Goals

This course has two simple but ambitious objectives: to help you think clearly, and to help you write clearly. Both of these are necessary for doing research in mathematics or anything else, but we will not assume that you have the knowledge or interest in mathematics needed to write theorems and proofs. Instead, we tackle puzzles and solutions, designed to be appreciated by anyone with an inquiring mind. We will talk about how to create puzzles and solve them, but more about how to write accurately and concisely, and to convey the essence of a puzzle and its solution to a reader.


Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

  1. State a problem clearly in writing;

  2. Frame a problem in ways which will intrigue and entertain a listener or reader;

  3. Explain a solution elegantly and concisely;

  4. Provide the tools to generalize a solution, so that readers may apply it elsewhere;

  5. Evaluate and criticize puzzle sources for clarity and taste.


The test: Can you present a puzzle to anyone (in particular, to a classmate) so that they grasp it immediately, and understand exactly what is asked without requiring clarification? Can you then present the solution with equal clarity? Can you explain how the solution was arrived at, and how it can be used on other problems? Can you describe what makes a great puzzle?


Pre-Requisites

As with all first-year seminars this one is designed to be taken immediately after Writing 5. There are no mathematical prerequisites and considerable variation in both mathematical background and problem-solving skills is anticipated.


Teaching Methods or Teaching Philosophy

Class will be conducted in workshop style, with students presenting puzzles and solutions to one another, both orally and in writing. With the guidance of the instructor, students will work on solving puzzles and on providing positive and negative feedback to fellow students’ writing. Puzzles will be selected from the literature by both instructor and students, and discussions often led by students. Very importantly, students will not be competing with each other to solve problems, but instead working together to find the best solutions, and to express their solutions in clear and economical manner.


Expectations

You are expected to attend all meetings of the class, to arrive on time, and to participate in class discussions and exercises. You will quickly discover that you need to put fears of giving wrong answers aside; in this class we are even more interested in the process of solving a problem than its solution. Of course you will be expected to hand in homework on time and to give thought to the expression of ideas as well as to the ideas themselves.

You will not be expected to solve puzzles instantly either at the beginning or the end of the course; but if you tend to panic when presented with a puzzle, or to have trouble organizing your thoughts and pursuing a promising line of inquiry, you can expect this course to help.

Text and Resources


Required:


The Colossal Book of Short Puzzles and Problems, by Martin Gardner, edited by Dana Richards, W.W. Norton & Co. 2005. List $35, Amazon $23.10.


How to Solve It, by George Polya, Foreward by Sam Sloan. Ishi Press 2009. List $25.95, Amazon $23.42.


Martin Gardner, a theologist by training, fascinated the public with mathematical puzzles for three decades by means of his Mathematical Games column in Scientific American magazine. All of his puzzles are collected in the volume above, as well as on a CD of all his columns which can be obtained from Amazon. We will be discussing selected (not all!) problems from this collection.

George Polya’s book is the acknowledged classic of texts on mathematical problem-solving.

There may be some additional, optional, puzzle books on reserve at the library.



Grading

The course grade will be based on class participation (25%), including problem-solving in groups and oral presentations to fellow students; and on written assignments (75%) in both draft and final forms. The grade will not be based on your ability to solve puzzles or to do mathematics. There will not be any quizzes or exams.


Academic Honor

Students are encouraged to communicate with one another on course topics outside as well as inside the classroom, but all written assignments must be completed individually. Papers must be exclusively the original work of the student and of properly cited sources.

Tentative Course Schedule: (May change to accommodate student needs)


The course will be divided into, roughly, 5 two-week sections, with a draft assignment due at the end of the first week of each section and a final version due at its conclusion

Section 1: What is a puzzle? What is it good for? Writing assignment: My favorite puzzle, and how to solve it. Reading: Gardner.

Day 1 (3/26): Lecture: what the course is about. Working groups: stating and solving combinatorial puzzles. Reading: Gardner I.1

Day 2 (3/28): Discussion: what use is a puzzle? Working groups: probability puzzles. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner I.2

Day 3 (4/2): Working groups: number theory. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner I.3

Day 4 (4/4): Discussion: how does stating a puzzle differ from other kinds of writing? Working groups: algebraic puzzles. Reading: Gardner I.4

Section II: What makes a great puzzle? Writing assignment: What is Gardner’s best puzzle, and why. Reading: Gardner.

Day 5 (4/9): Discussion: why do you like a puzzle? Working groups: plane geometry. Reading: Gardner II.1

Day 6 (4/11): Discussion: where are puzzles found? Working groups: solid geometry. Reading: Gardner II.2, puzzle websites

Day 7 (4/16): Working groups: shapes and dissections. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner II.3, puzzle websites

Day 8 (4/18): Working groups: Topological reasoning. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner II.4, puzzle websites

Section III: How do we solve puzzles, and how should we? Writing assignment: A puzzle-solving technique, how and when to apply it. Reading: Polya.

Day 9 (4/23): Discussion: Puzzles about algorithms, and algorithms for solving puzzles. Reading: Gardner III.1, Polya Forward & Introduction

Day 10 (4/25): Working groups: scheduling and planning. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner III.2, Polya Part I

Day 11 (4/30): Working groups: games people don’t play. Reading: Gardner III.3, Polya Part II & some of Part III

Day 12 (5/2): Working groups: Logic puzzles. Student presentations. Reading: Gardner IV.1, Polya Part III

Section IV: How do you create a puzzle? How do you turn a mathematical exercise into a seductive problem? Writing assignment: Two settings for an abstract puzzle. Reading: Gardner.

Day 13 (5/7): Lecture: from abstraction to reality. Discussion: creating a setting

Day 14 (5/9): Working groups: puzzle setting. Reading: Gardner Appendix

Day 15 (5/14): Student presentations. Reading: puzzle websites.

Day 16 (5/16): Working groups: polishing a presentation. Reading: Polya IV

Section V: How do you classify puzzles? How can they be grouped for ease of presentation or solution? How do you find source information on a puzzle or category of puzzles? Writing assignment: A type of puzzle, examples, history, methods for attack. Reading: library and web.

Day 17 (5/21): Lecture and discussion: classification ideas. Reading: Gardner review.

Day 18 (5/23): Working groups: classifying Gardner’s puzzles. Reading: Gardner review.

Day 19 (5/28): Discussion and working groups: polishing the final assignment.
